# Media Studies & AI Learning Notes
## File 1: Making New Media Make Sense (Nancy K. Baym)
### Core Objective
- Exploring how scholars and users make sense of new communication technologies by balancing technological features with personal, cultural, and historical presumptions.
---
### I. Theoretical Frameworks of Causality
Baym identifies four primary lenses through which we understand the relationship between technology and society:
* **Technological Determinism:** **Concept:** Technology is an external agent that acts upon and changes society with little human resistance.
    * **Variants:** Technologies transfer their "essential qualities" to users (e.g., Google "making us stupid" or TV causing short attention spans).
        * *Media Choice:* A milder form where users make rational choices based on a medium's features to fulfill specific tasks.
    * **Binary Rhetoric:** Usually oscillates between **Utopian** (technology as a natural improvement/liberator) and **Dystopian** (technology as an agent of doom/loss of control).
* **Social Construction of Technology (SCOT):**
    * **Concept:** People, not machines, are the primary sources of change.
    * **Factors:** Invention is influenced by social contexts, investors, competitors, and government agencies.
    * **User Influence:** Users often subvert or modify technologies for unintended purposes (e.g., the Internet moving from a military system to a social tool).
* **Social Shaping of Technology:**
    * **Concept:** A middle ground where influence flows in both directions.
    * **Affordances:** Technologies provide specific capabilities (logics), but the actual consequences arise from a mix of these features and the emergent ways people use them.
* **Domestication:**
    * **Concept:** The process by which technology moves from being "wild" or strange to a "tame," taken-for-granted part of everyday life.
    * **Result:** The technology becomes invisible (e.g., the alphabet or landline telephones (now cellphones)).
---
### II. Recurring Themes in New Media Reception
* **Authenticity:** A persistent fear that mediated interactions are "not real" or lack the "aura" of face-to-face connection.
* **Anonymity:** Viewed as a binary of **Honesty/Liberation** (freedom from social markers like race/gender) vs. **Deception** (fear of "dogs" or liars online).
* **Quality of Interaction:** Concerns that digital media lead to "pointless babble" or shallow connections.
* **Effects on Relationships:**
    * *Utopian:* Finding niche communities and expanding social circles.
    * *Dystopian:* The "homewrecker" narrative (cyberaffairs) and social isolation (becoming a "recluse").
---
### III. The "Moral Panic"
* **Focus:** Often centers on the protection of children and teenage girls.
* **Mechanism:** Anxieties over uncontrollable social forces are displaced onto technology because it is an easier target than fixing underlying social institutions (homes, schools, etc.).
* **Paradox:** While children are seen as victims, they are also seen as "dangerous" because their technical mastery exceeds that of their parents.

## File 2: The Myth of Automated Learning (Nicholas Carr)
### Core Argument
- Generative AI is an automation technology that poses a threat to education not because it encourages cheating, but because it **discourages the work of learning**.
---
### I. The Three Scenarios of Automation
When a machine automates a task, one of three things happens to the human user's skill:
1.  **Growth:** If the user is already a master, the machine handles routine tasks, allowing the user to tackle harder challenges (e.g., a mathematician using a calculator).
2.  **Atrophy (Skill Fade):** If a master stops practicing the manual skill, they get "rusty" and lose situational awareness (e.g., pilots over-relying on autopilot).
3.  **Stillborn Skill (Deskilling):** When a person uses a machine before gaining direct experience, the skill never develops. This is the primary risk for students using AI.
---
### II. The Product vs. The Experience
* **The Decoupling:** AI allows students to produce the **product** (the paper/assignment) without the **experience** (the work of learning).
* **The Learning Process:** Real learning happens during the "heavy lifting"—critical reading, synthesis of ideas, and the struggle to express thoughts in sentences.
* **The Proxy Problem:** Grades are meant to assess the *effort and thought* behind the work. AI makes the assignment a "substitute for the work" rather than evidence of learning.
---
### III. Consequences for Students
* **Illusion of Learning:** Students may produce "A" quality work with a chatbot while their internal capability drops to a "C" level.
* **The Prompting Paradox:** Using AI effectively requires a deep understanding of the subject to write good prompts; however, relying on AI prevents that understanding from developing.
* **The Cycle of Dependency:**
    * Students feel "lazier" and lose the ability to think critically.
    * A sense of "sadness" or "loss of motivation" occurs as they feel they are learning nothing.
    * Social pressure ("Everyone is doing it") forces further adoption.
---
### IV. Institutional Perspective
* **Economic Interest:** For AI companies, students are viewed as **customers**, not learners. Providing free access (like ChatGPT Plus for students) ensures long-term dependency.
* **Educational Foundation:** The logic of written assignments is falling apart because the writing can now be decoupled from the thought process.
