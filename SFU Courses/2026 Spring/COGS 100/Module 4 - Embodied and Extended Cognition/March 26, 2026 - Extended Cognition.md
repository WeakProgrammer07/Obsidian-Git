- How, if at all, does extended cognition challenge Classical CogSci?
	- The idea that cognitive processes involve objects and people in the physical environment is not necessarily anti-representational.
	- But it does challenge the implicit assumption of Classical CogSci that the mind/brain is the sole site of cognition.
		- Implicit assumption: an idea that Classical CogSci theorists rely on to make their case, even if they don’t explicitly say it’s part of their theory.
		- Sole site of cognition: Cognitive processes are those which happen in the mind/brain – the mind/brain is the central computer, doing the computational work involved in thought and action.
	- So, you could hold that cognition is representational, but also think that cognition is extended. But, you can’t hold that cognition is extended and also think that cognitive processes only happen in the mind/brain.
- Extended Cognition: Empirical testing
	- Linda Smith & Esther Thelen (1996):
		- In infants, babies, and toddlers, learning doesn't happen without interaction with the environment
			- Can't learn to walk without movement and physical scaffolds result in different walking processes
		- Even if features of environment are inconsistent, that doesn’t mean they aren’t essential
	- Luis Favela and Anthony Chemero (2016):
		- Definition of Extended Cognition: A cognitive task is considered "extended" when the system incorporates features of the external world.
		- Scope of Tasks: This applies to active processes such as:
		    - Decision making
		    - Goal-directed behaviour
		- Spatial Boundaries: It involves elements located beyond the traditional physical limits of an organism, such as:
		    - Skin
		    - Shells
		    - Scales
		- Functional Integration: The external "tools" (like a notebook, a calculator, or a smartphone) function as part of the thinking loop, rather than just being objects the brain observes.
	- Luis Favela, Mary Jean Amon, Lorena Lobo, Anthony Chemero (2021): 
		- Tools allow people to more flexibly engage and think about the world. We can quantify this! Physical objects are just as essential to creativity as our own capacity for representation or mental modelling
- Extended Cognition: Criticisms
	- Two main objections have been targeted at extended mind:
		- There’s a distinction between constitution and coupling.
		- Otto doesn’t have the right kind of knowledge.
	- Fred Adams & Kenneth Aizawa (2008):
		- There’s a difference between coupling and constitution.
			- Coupled systems = distinct systems which are connected to one another
				- My phone and my AirPods - they are two distinct apparatuses which work together for some purpose (letting me listen to music)
			- Constitutive parts = parts which make up a system
				- My phone’s screen partially constitutes (makes up) my phone.
	- Otto’s notebook is a coupled system to Otto’s own mind. It is not part of Otto’s mind, but it works with Otto’s mind for certain ends (getting him to the VanArt Gallery).
	- The ‘mark of the mental’ objection:
		- The knowledge Otto gets is not from the right kind of source or not the right kind of knowledge.
		- Otto is not connected to his knowledge in the same way Inga is.
- Extended Cognition: It depends on functionalism
	- Some have argued that extended cognition is only compelling if you accept functionalism about cognition
	- Functionalism: 
		- What makes something a mental (or cognitive) state is its relation to other states.
	- Whether Otto’s actions amount to memory retrieval depends on a functionalist account of memory, not on some sort of argument about the physical processes which must be involved in memory
	- For extended cognition theorists, it’s not about the specific kind of body or its parts which do the thinking… it’s about the behaviours which are produced (and whether or not those behaviours play the appropriate functional role).
	- If extended cognition theories depend on an appeal to functionalism, then to accept extended cognition, we would have to:
		- Accept that there is no functional difference between Otto and Inga, and
		- Accept that functionalism about cognition is true (that the particular function of a state is all that matters for calling it a cognitive state).
