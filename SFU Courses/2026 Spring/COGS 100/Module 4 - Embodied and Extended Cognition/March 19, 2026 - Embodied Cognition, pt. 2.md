- The main innovation of the embodied approach is the way it evaluates the significance of the body in cognition. This leads us to think about mental processes beyond computational (informational/representational) processes.
- We can contrast how specific mental processes are described on the classical picture versus on the embodied picture
- The classical CogSci approach to vision: 
	- We intake visual stimuli and organize those stimuli into representations of visual objects.
		- Step 1. Perceive (see the apple)
		- Step 2. Represent (mental model of the apple)
		- Step 3. Use the representation to guide your action (pick up the apple with the proper grasp, eat the apple, etc.
	- The classical picture is about building and manipulating representations.
	- Your actions are mediated through representations.
- Some embodied cognition theorists have used change blindness to challenge this classical picture.
	- Change blindness is the inattentional phenomenon where there is a failure to perceive real-time changes in an object.
- Embodied cognitive theorists use change blindness to suggest that perception is fragmentary.
	- We often aren’t perceiving full, stable, unchanging objects (and building representations of those objects). We’re perceiving moving and changing objects, which exist in a dynamic context
	- If our perception is fragmentary, and if we aren’t perceiving full, stable objects, but we’re nevertheless able to move through the world and act appropriately, what does this mean for the role of representation in our cognition?
- What does this mean for the role of representation in our cognition? Either:
	- There are no internal representations.
		- Classical CogSci:
			- Perception → Representation → Action
		- Embodied CogSci:
			- Perception → Action
		- Cognition is not mediated by representations.
	- We only sometimes represent things – representational cognition is secondary to direct, embodied cognition.
	- We can represent things in special cases:
		- Remembering
			- I can represent the path from here to Renaissance cafe, even when it isn’t immediately perceptually available to me.
		- Manipulating
			- I can represent what this shirt would look like if it were red instead of blue.
	- Such cases of representation are secondary to direct actionable perception in the world.
		- I walk from here to Renaissance.
		- I choose the red shirt and put it on
- How do we learn to navigate the world without representations guiding our action?
	- James Gibson (1979):
		- “The affordances of the environment are what it offers the animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or ill.”
	- Affordances:
		- Opportunities for acting in the world which are determined by an animal’s bodily makeup and capacity.
	- Affordances help us understand:
		- How our specific bodies shape our perception and action in the world.
		- How we learn to respond appropriately to objects in the world based on our bodies, not necessarily based on representations.
	- If the embodied view is correct, then much of our cognition stems directly from and is directly shaped by our bodily contact with the world.
	- Many embodied cognition theorists have used this idea to argue for a stronger claim: Physical bodies are a necessary condition for cognition.
- Our paradigmatic physical bodies for thinking about cognition are biological bodies: organic, made up of living matter, connected with natural processes, self-organized
	- Biological bodies are the ones thought to have environmental goals (things they want to do in the environment) and bodies which have adapted to those environments (based on perceived affordances).
- But what about non-biological physical bodies
	-  Artificial bodies are almost always capable of processing just one sensory modality (they just touch or hear, whereas humans combine multiple modalities).
	-  They lack particular goals or aims in their environmental interactions, or at least, their goals are not self-directed
		- And as a result, their interactions with the environment are not meaningful or otherwise significant to them

Position: I think that it is possible to have cognition without requiring a living body. 

One example, although currently might be lacking but will likely change in the future, is robotics. Cognition is the process of representing information, processing those representations, and guiding action through representations, which all can be done via a multitude of sensors. Especially with the AI models that we currently have, they can follow a loop, perceiving, acting, evaluating, reasoning, all of what a human currently does. There was also a case recently where scientists were able to get a fly into a simulation, a normal fly brain perceiving, looking for food, doing everything a normal fly would but with a digital body. If these are not considered some level of cognition then I do not think it is possible to consider humans or other biological creatures as having cognition either, note that this is not consciousness.

I will use robotics as the example, the human body is a bunch of sensors and components that all come together to figure out how to operate in the world. This can be seen in robots, while some may be missing some features, some humans are also missing features. The human body is nothing special, as technology advances we will likely be able to simulate all of the features. Another way to look at it is saying does a person who is paralyzed, blind, deaf, and only able to speak due to technology less cognitive then a person that is able to sense? This makes it seem like the body is not the requirement for cognition. Maybe in the far future we will be able to clone the brain exactly, making it no different from a human in any way.
