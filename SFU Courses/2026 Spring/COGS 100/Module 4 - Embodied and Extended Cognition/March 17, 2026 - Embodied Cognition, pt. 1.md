- Cognition
	- On the standard or classical view in Cognitive Science, cognition is the process of representing information, processing those representations, and guiding action through representations
	- Image processing is needed for cognition
- Consciousness
	- GWT
		- Consciousness is subjective experience
		- Attention is necessary for consciousness
	- IIT
		- Consciousness is information. Conscious experience is subjective experience
		- Consciousness is everywhere. A sufficient amount of consciousness, arranged in the right way, is necessary for conscious experience
- Embodied and extended cognition are part of what’s called the 4E approach to studying cognition
- 4E approach
	1. Embodied: shaped by the body
	2. Extended: not limited by individual bodies
	3. Embedded: shaped by the environment
	4. Enactive: shaped by action
- Will only focus on Embodied and Extended in this class
- Questions about cognition:
	- What kinds of processes are cognitive processes?
		- What's the difference between cognition and other kinds of biological or physical processes? (E.g. muscle reflexes vs remembering -> the former is not cognitive but the latter is)
	- What causes cognition?
		- What makes cognitive processing happen? What brings about cognitive processing? (E.g.  exposure to an olfactory stimulus and attention cause smelling)
	- What constitutes cognition?
		- What is cognition made out of? What are the ‘parts’ of cognition? What are the necessary features of cognition? (E.g., Olfaction is made out of the olfactory tract; Representation is necessary for cognition)
	- When Cognitive Scientists typically answer these questions, their answers are too limited
- Classical CogSci:
	- What kinds of processes are cognitive?
		- Those involving representation or information processing
	- What causes cognition?
		- The brain receiving stimuli as informational input
	- What constitutes cognition?
		- The brain (and its parts and processes); conscious experience
	- What we’re going to talk about in Module 4 is a rejection of the classical view of cognition that we’ve been working with so far in this course.
- Points of tension between Classical CogSci and the 4E approach:
	- Is cognition really just representational information processing?
	- Does cognition occur only or primarily in the (human) brain?
	- Are individual brains or bodies the boundary of cognition?
- Embodied cognition
	- Embodied cognition theorists make a few important claims:
	1. The brain is not the primary seat of cognition.
		- In classical cognition it all happens in the brain
		- In embodied cognition is a full body process
		- This is a claim about what constitutes cognition and about what counts as a cognitive process
	2. The body plays a fundamental role in shaping cognition.
		- How do we learn how to grasp objects of different shapes and sizes?
			- Classical CogSci: we represent objects, categorize those representations based on features of the objects (small, square, etc.), and do the action which corresponds to the correct representation of an object.
			- Embodied CogSci: We feel objects and develop spatial, sensory, and physical categories which determine our interactions.
		- Why do bats use sound to represent distance? Why do dogs use smell to represent distance? Why do humans (primarily) use vision to represent distance? 
			- Classical CogSci: Doesn’t really have a satisfying answer.
			- Embodied CogSci: Different bodies have different abilities. These different abilities make for a different character of perception.
		- This is a claim about what constitutes cognition and what causes cognition to unfold in a particular way
	3. Bodies are necessary for cognition.
		- Why do we think?
			- To navigate our world.
		- How do we think?
			- By intaking information about our world.
		- This is a claim about what causes cognition.
		- Embodied CogSci: You neither can think nor need to think without a body capable of sensory experience and intaking sensory information.
		- This claim is controversial, because it’s not clear what kinds of bodies are necessary for cognition.
			- Is a robot that moves through the world using sensors the right kind of body?
