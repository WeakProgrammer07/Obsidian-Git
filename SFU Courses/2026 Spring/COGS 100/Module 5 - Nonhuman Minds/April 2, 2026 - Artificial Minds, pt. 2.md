- Imitation vs. Uniqueness
    - Acting like a human doesn’t necessarily mean a machine actually has a mind.
    - Passing the Turing Test only proves a machine can _talk_ like a human, not _think_ like one.
        - It just shows the machine can string words together exactly like we do.    
- Linguistic indistinguishability: Talking the same way.
- Cognitive indistinguishability: Thinking the same way.
- People separate talking from thinking to show that **copying behaviour** doesn't equal having a real mind.
    - Just copying behaviour isn't enough to prove a mind exists.
    - Acting human isn't proof of thinking like a human.
- It's easy for machines to fake human behaviour, but the background process isn't real thinking.
    - For example, a chatbot doesn't actually understand what its words mean in the real world.
- As AI gets better at faking it, we are realizing that "smart-looking" results don't always come from actually "smart" processes.
- Denis Diderot (1746): Said if a parrot could answer any question, he'd consider it completely intelligent.
    - That made sense back then, but we think differently now.
- Real minds are defined by _how_ they work behind the scenes, not just _what_ they do on the outside.
    - This includes things like actually understanding information, experiencing the world (senses, memory), and having consciousness or emotions.
        - Without these inner experiences, outward behaviour doesn't mean much; inner experience is what makes a mind.
- True thinking can happen in many ways. Judging minds purely by "how well they imitate humans" is too narrow.
- Deep Blue's (the chess AI) methods were different from ours, but they didn't seem any less intelligent, strategic, or complex.
    - This raises a question: Can completely different underlying processes create completely different kinds of minds?
- From this, we have two distinct reasons to reject the idea that a mind is just about imitating human behaviour:
    - **What you need to have a mind are certain representational, perceptual, and consciousness-related capacities:** 
	    - A true mind needs the ability to understand the world, perceive things, and be conscious. Humans have these traits naturally. Just faking human behaviour doesn’t prove a machine actually has these inner experiences.
    - **Cognition comes in all shapes and sizes:** 
	    - Intelligence comes in all shapes and sizes. Judging a mind purely by how closely it copies (imitates) humans is too narrow of a standard.
- There is a clear problem with the idea that minds can come in all shapes and sizes.
	- If a mind can look like literally anything, what does the word even mean? And how can scientists possibly study something that has no set definition?
	- Response: All minds must share a few core, basic features. As long as it has those core features, the rest of the mind can look totally different depending on the animal or machine.
