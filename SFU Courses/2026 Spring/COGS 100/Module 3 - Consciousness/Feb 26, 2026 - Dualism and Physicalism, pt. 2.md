- Science is set up to study physical substances
	- If dualism is true then we are unable to study the mind scientifically
- The Knowledge Argument
	- Thought Experiment, Mary's Room
		- **The Argument:** If Mary knows every **physical** fact about "red" but learns something new (the **experience** of red) upon leaving the room, then physical facts do not account for everything in the universe.
	    - **The Goal:** To prove the existence of **Qualia**, subjective "raw feels" that science cannot fully explain through biology or physics alone
	- If there are non-physical properties to consciousness experience, then something like dualism must be true
- Responses to the Knowledge Argument
	- The Explanatory Gap
		- There is a gap between consciousness and the physical brain states. Facts about conscious mental states cannot be explained by facts about the corresponding physical states.
		- Consciousness might arise from physical states, but there is something about consciousness that we struggle to capture with standard explanations
		- Explanatory gap theorists are worried that we lack the concepts needed to bridge objective explanations with subjective experiences
	- Dualism
		- We can’t have scientific explanations of consciousness because consciousness is a non-physical substance.
		- Dualists are worried about of what kind of thing consciousness is
- Easy Problems of Consciousness
	- Identifying and describing the physical processes in the brain which constitute the objective view
- Hard Problem of Consciousness
	- Figuring out how the physical processes give rise to the subjective experience of consciousness.
- "What it is like to be a bat"
	- Main Ideas
		- Subjective experience, or ‘what it is like-ness’ is necessary for consciousness
		- The subjective quality and subjective access of ‘what it is like-ness’ make these features of consciousness impossible to study objectively (or scientifically)
			- Because we cannot access (imagine) what it is like to be a bat, the subjective character of experience is essentially first-personal and inaccessible for objective inquiry
	- Objections:
		- Is this just a problem of the limits of our imagination? And not a problem with the essential nature of ‘what it is like-ness’?
		- A bat is very different from us! Isn’t it easier to imagine what it’s like to be Dr. Anwarzai than what it’s like to be a bat? Maybe we can get insight about others’ consciousness, so long as those others are similar enough creatures to us
