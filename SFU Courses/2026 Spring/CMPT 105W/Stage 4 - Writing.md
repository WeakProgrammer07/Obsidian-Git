### Intro
In recent years we have seen facial recognition technology transition from a futuristic concept to become something that is seemingly everywhere, deployed by not only law enforcement but also private entities. Advocates of facial recognition often point to how it is able to help identify suspects and solve criminal cases. However, underneath all of this success is a deeply flawed system which infringes upon the privacy of individuals and even when it appears to be working is often biased against minorities. This technology is able to give entities an unprecedented amount of power to monitor and control people, without giving people any options. Facial recognition has already been used in controlling ways, one such example is how protesters are being identified and then prevented from protesting again in Russia (CIVICUS, 2025). Due to the unregulated nature, the clear algorithmic biases, and its harmful capabilities if misused, facial recognition should be banned from usage in public spaces to protect our privacy and freedoms. 

### Supporting Arguments
The debate over facial recognition in public spaces is based on the question of how individuals privacy and civil liberties belong in public places. When individuals can be tracked, identified, and catalogued without their knowledge or consent, the fundamental right to privacy is destroyed, making a ban the only effective safeguard. 
The constant scanning of crowds capturing the biometric data of innocent civilians without their explicit consent, knowledge, or even the reasonable expectation of being tracked (Hirose, 2017). Unlike normal surveillance, which might record that "someone" was at a location, facial recognition changes this, transforming anonymity into a permanent identity. Even though individuals are in a public space they are not consenting to being permanently tracked and put into databases for either known or unknown purposes. This data might be sold to private entities, subpoenaed by the government, or even leaked through security issues, all without the subject knowing anything. Either deploying or keeping this technology deployed is incompatible with the expectation and right to some degree of privacy in shared spaces.  
The technology's flaws extend beyond privacy. Facial recognition systems exhibit severe algorithmic bias. Looking more into this technology we can see it is lacking, studies on gender classification algorithms, like what would be used in facial recognition algorithms, shows significant accuracy disparities. These studies showed that darker skinned females were being misidentified at much higher rates compared to those of lighter skinned males. (Buolamwini & Gebru, 2018) Specifically, the research found error rates of up to 34.7% for darker skinned women versus only 0.8% for light skinned males, a disparity that is more than insignificant. In the real world this means that a Black woman walking through a public place that is monitored by a facial recognition software is about 40 times more likely to be falsely flagged as a suspect compared to a white male walking beside her. When law enforcement acts on these flawed identifications it can be potentially devastating based on an algorithmic error and not even probable cause. Deploying these biased algorithms into a production environment with the current biases inevitable leads to the unjust targeting, harassment, or even false arrest of marginalized groups. A technology this discriminatory is too dangerous and harmful for use in public. 
There are more dangers though, this technology has the possibility of threatening the foundations of democratic society. Civil liberty organizations argue that attempting to regulate the technology is futile because any level of mass biometric tracking creates a chilling effect on free speech and peaceful assembly. (Marissa & Cagle, 2024) When citizens of democratic societies know that their identities can be instantly captured and logged at protests, or gatherings, they disengage from the society and life. Surveillance even when deployed for specific security purposes inevitably expands towards political control. Facial recognition transforms public places from places of anonymous dissent, like peaceful protests, into monitored zones where participation carries a permanent consequence. Because this technology can function as the ultimate tool for government overreach merely regulating its use is quite insufficient. The only way to preserve the civil liberties we possess is to ban the technology. 
All three of these are demonstrating how facial recognition technology is incompatible with our society and should thus be banned.
### Counter Arguments
Despite the substantial risks outlined above, there are people who believe that facial recognition technology is the future and bring arguments in its defense. To have a good understanding of the topic these arguments must be considered as to see why facial recognition is even being considered in public spaces. 
Those who are proponents of facial recognition often emphasize its effectiveness in police enforcement and solving crimes. Police forces have reported significant successes, such as the Metropolitan Police attributing a thousand arrests directly to the use of facial recognition cameras (Jessup, 2025). This statistic suggests that the technology is basically a force multiplier for these police departments, allowing them to do more with less. There are flaws with this though and with scrutiny we can see some of the pitfalls in this argument. First, the raw arrest numbers do not reflect the accuracy or justice, many of the arrests may have been based on errors or false identifications. Second, this may come at the cost of civil liberties which is unacceptable in our society. A police state may have super high arrest rates through facial recognition, but democratic states reject this because the process and respecting individual rights are an important part of democracy. Finally, looking at the previous biases presented we can infer that these thousand arrests likely include a disproportionate number of false positives from marginalized communities. Efficiency should not justify violations of privacy and liberties, nor can it ignore the harm inflicted on innocent individuals involved. 
Some argue that banning facial recognition is not needed and excessive. Research into the intersection of big data and ethics suggests that robust data protection laws, algorithmic auditing, and strict usage guidelines can safely manage biometric surveillance (Wang et al., 2024). This research brings us to the conclusion that it might be reactionary to ban the tool outright. By implementing strict legal boundaries, society may benefit from the security aspects of the technology while mitigating the privacy risks. There are some historical issues and current issues that show that this is naive.  Civil society organizations document how this technology is increasingly deployed to suppress dissent, with protesters being identified and prevented from exercising their rights to assembly and free expression (CIVICUS, 2025). The CIVICUS research reveals that facial recognition has become "the latest weapon against civil society," used by authorities to monitor, track, and intimidate activists regardless of existing regulatory frameworks. Governments are also unable to guarantee with certainty that this data is safe and secure, as well as being unsure if a future government will change the way that the data is used. 
Lastly the proponents advocate that banning facial recognition would leave security infrastructure outdated. Modern data analysis and big data frameworks allow for the rapid processing of security information and threat detection at a scale that human operators simply cannot match (Wang et al., 2024). As cities grow and threats evolve, security systems must adapt. Banning advanced artificial intelligence tools leaves public safety stuck in the past, making society vulnerable to modern threats that require rapid, automated responses. Yet this argument mistakes outdated with unsafe, societies have maintained public safety for decades without mass surveillance. The issues that facial recognition might address, terrorism, organized crime, are also the scenarios where false positives and errors have the most consequence, potentially creating more chaos instead of preventing it. Reliance on biased algorithms is not progress, it is a retreat into the illusion that technology is the solution to all of our problems. 
Efficiency, regulation, and modernization may sound like the future, but they cannot erase the more important issues, privacy, bias, and the threats to democracy.
### Conclusion
Facial Recognition clearly is usable to enhance the efficiency of law enforcement, leading to more arrests and identifying suspects. But the fundamental risks that it poses to our current society far outweigh those benefits. The technology clearly suffers from algorithmic bias, risking to harm already marginalized communities. It also removes any reasonable expectation of privacy is spaces, creating an infrastructure that is a prime target for harmful entities. Regulation cannot contain the threat of mass surveillance, so implementing a total ban is the only solution. This is more than just a privacy issue now. When facial tracking becomes normalized, democratic participation itself becomes a liability, citizens distance themselves, protests dwindle, and accountability disappears. Moving forward, policy makers must reject the false choice between security and liberty, recognizing that true safety requires systems accountable to citizens, not algorithms that monitor them. The future we choose depends on banning facial recognition now, before its infrastructure becomes too entrenched to dismantle.
