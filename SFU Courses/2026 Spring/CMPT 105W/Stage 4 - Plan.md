 Essay Structure (1000-1500 words)

  1. Title Page (APA Student Style)
	  - Title of your essay
	  - Your name, SFU affiliation
	  - Course name (CMPT 105W), instructor name (Diana Cukierman)
	  - Date (2026)
  2. Introduction (~150-200 words)
	  - Hook: Start with the transition of facial recognition from sci-fi to surveillance reality
	  - Context: Current deployment by law enforcement and private entities; the tension between security and privacy
	  - Thesis: "Due to the unregulated nature, clear algorithmic biases, and harmful capabilities if misused, facial recognition should be banned from usage in public spaces to protect our privacy and freedoms."
  3. Supporting Arguments (~500-600 words)
	  Structure each body paragraph as: Topic → Evidence → Explanation
	  Paragraph 1: Privacy Violation
	  - Claim: Constant biometric scanning captures data of innocent civilians without consent
	  - Evidence: Hirose (2017) on biometric tracking without knowledge or reasonable expectation
	  - Warrant: Even in public, permanent cataloging violates fundamental privacy rights
	  Paragraph 2: Algorithmic Bias
	  - Claim: Commercial facial recognition shows significant accuracy disparities
	  - Evidence: Buolamwini & Gebru (2018) – darker-skinned females misidentified at much higher rates
	  - Warrant: Deploying biased tools leads to unjust targeting of marginalized groups
	  Paragraph 3: Civil Liberties Threat
	  - Claim: Mass biometric tracking creates chilling effects on free speech and assembly
	  - Evidence: Marissa & Cagle (2024) – regulation is futile; any mass tracking enables government overreach
	  - Warrant: A ban is the only way to protect civil liberties
  4. Counter-Arguments & Rebuttals (~250-300 words)
	  Counter-Argument 1: Law Enforcement Efficiency
	  - Present: Metropolitan Police reported 1,000 arrests from facial recognition (Jessup, 2025)
	  - Rebuttal: Efficiency gains don't justify rights violations; biased arrests harm innocent people
	  Counter-Argument 2: Regulation as Alternative
	  - Present: Wang et al. (2024) – robust data protection and auditing can manage risks
	  - Rebuttal: History shows regulation fails; technology outpaces lawmakers; ban is the only safeguard
  5. Conclusion (~150-200 words)
	  - Summarize: Recap privacy, bias, and civil liberties concerns
	  - "So What?": Without a ban, we normalize mass surveillance; democracy requires privacy and freedom from tracking
	  - What's Next: Call to action for policymakers to prioritize rights over convenience
  6. References (APA format, not included in word count)
  Include at least:
  - Buolamwini & Gebru (2018)
  - Hirose (2017)
  - Marissa & Cagle (2024)
  - Jessup (2025)
  - Wang et al. (2024)
