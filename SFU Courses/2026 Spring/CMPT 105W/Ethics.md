### **Kantian Ethics and Deontological Theories**
- Deontological theories assert that features of an action other than its consequences determine its moral rightness or wrongness.
- Kant’s approach is a strong deontological theory, meaning moral assessment operates entirely independently of good or bad consequences.
- Categorical imperatives represent how reason commands the will and are the only possible moral imperatives.
- The Universalizability rule states that you should only act on moral rules that everyone else could follow without creating a logical contradiction.
- The Humanity rule states that it is wrong to "use" another person, and every interaction must treat individuals with the dignity and respect they deserve as autonomous, rational beings.
### **Case Application: Browsing In-Store but Buying Online**
- Under Kantian rules, this behavior is considered unethical
- Applying universalizability: If everyone browsed in stores but bought online, physical stores would earn no income and close, resulting in no open stores left for customers to browse, which creates a logical contradiction.
- Applying humanity: By hiding their intention not to buy, the customer fails to provide the whole truth to store employees, thereby using them as a means to an end rather than treating them as autonomous and rational humans.
### **Utilitarianism and Consequentialist Theories**
- Consequentialist theories base judgments of obligation on judgments of value, meaning they instruct us to pursue things identified as good or valuable, such as freedom.
- These judgments focus on what properties are good rather than directly assessing the actions themselves.
- The Principle of Utility (Greatest Happiness principle) dictates that an action is right or wrong based on the extent to which it increases or decreases the total happiness of all affected parties.
- Act utilitarianism applies the principle of utility to specific actions in the short run, calculating the total change in happiness across all affected beings to choose the action that maximizes total happiness.
- Rule utilitarianism applies the principle of utility to rules rather than short-term actions, advocating for the adoption of moral rules that would yield the greatest increase in total happiness in the long run if everyone followed them.
### **Case Application: Hijacked Plane**
- This case questions if it is morally right to shoot down a hijacked aircraft carrying innocent people to save a greater number of lives.
- The scenario is used to explore how a consequentialist theory, specifically Act versus Rule utilitarianism, would evaluate the morality of the action based on the outcomes.
