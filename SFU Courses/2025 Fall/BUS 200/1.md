# Briefing on Business Fundamentals and Economic Systems

## Executive Summary

The central thesis is that all businesses operate within a dynamic environment shaped by fundamental economic forces, government influence, and competitive pressures. Success hinges on a manager's ability to navigate the challenges and opportunities presented by these forces.

Key takeaways include:

- **Economic Systems:** A nation's economic system determines how it allocates resources (factors of production: labour, capital, entrepreneurs, natural resources, and information). Systems range from **command economies** (communism, socialism), where government has central control, to **market economies** (capitalism), where individual producers and consumers make decisions via supply and demand. Most nations, including Canada, operate as **mixed market economies**, blending elements of both.

The government acts as a customer, competitor (via Crown corporations), regulator (through the Competition Act), taxation agent, and provider of incentives and essential services. Competition, a cornerstone of private enterprise, exists in four main degrees: perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and monopoly. These structures dictate a firm's ability to influence price and strategy.

## The Nature and Goals of Business

A **business** is defined as an organization that seeks to earn profits by providing goods and services. These entities produce the majority of goods and services consumed in Canada, employ most of the workforce, and contribute significant tax revenue to support government functions. The ultimate goal is to generate **profit**, which is the amount remaining after all business expenses are subtracted from revenues. Profit serves as the reward for owners who risk their time and capital. For instance, the _Hunger Games_ film series generated approximately $3 billion in gross revenues, yielding large profits for Lionsgate, its production company.

In contrast, **not-for-profit organizations** (e.g., charities, hospitals, educational institutions) use funds from grants or sales to provide public services rather than to generate profit for owners.

In 2019, Forbes ranked Canada as the sixth-best country for ease of conducting business transactions, trailing the United Kingdom, Sweden, Hong Kong, the Netherlands, and New Zealand.

## Global Economic Systems and Factors of Production

The operation of a business is fundamentally determined by the **economic system** of its host country. An economic system is the way in which a nation allocates its resources among its citizens. The primary distinction between systems lies in how they manage the **factors of production**.

### The Five Factors of Production

1. **Labour:** The mental and physical capabilities of the people who work for a company. Also known as human resources.
2. **Capital:** The funds required to start, operate, and grow a business. Sources include owner investments, stock sales, profits, and loans.
3. **Entrepreneurs:** Individuals who accept the risks and opportunities involved in creating and operating businesses. Examples include Tobias Lütke (Shopify) and Rihanna (Fenty Beauty).
4. **Natural Resources:** All physical resources, such as land, water, mineral deposits, and trees. Canada is rich in natural resources, demonstrated by major transactions like West Fraser Timber's $4 billion bid for Norbord and Cenovus Energy's $3.8 billion purchase of Husky Energy.
5. **Information:** The specialized knowledge, expertise, and economic data that businesses use. Unlike other factors, information can be shared without being diminished.

## Case Study: The COVID-19 Pandemic as the Ultimate Disruption

The COVID-19 pandemic stands as one of the most significant economic disruptions in the last century, sending shockwaves through the global economy and fundamentally altering business operations and consumer behavior.

### The K-Shaped Recovery

The pandemic's economic impact was not uniform, leading to what is described as a "K-shaped recovery." Certain sectors experienced sharp declines while others saw exponential growth.

**Industries in Decline:**

- **Travel and Hospitality:** The hotel industry in Canada was down an estimated 62.8% in 2020. Air Canada reported an 88% decline in activity.
- **Live Events and Entertainment:** Restaurants, small retail, live concerts, and movie theaters were among the hardest-hit sectors.

**Industries That Thrived:**

- **Technology:** As work and education moved online, Zoom's stock price surged by 355% in Q2 2020. Shopify's revenues increased dramatically as businesses expanded their online presence.
- **Home Improvement and Renovation:** With travel restricted, consumers "cocooned" at home. Home Depot's same-store sales climbed by 23.4%, and Canada's softwood lumber exports increased substantially to meet demand for renovations and swimming pools.

The pandemic underscored the necessity for managers and entrepreneurs to adapt rapidly, minimize costs, and find new revenue streams to survive extreme market disruptions.

## Business-Government Interaction in the Canadian Context

In Canada's mixed market economy, the relationship between business and government is multifaceted and interactive.

### How Government Influences Business

- **As a Customer:** Government is a major purchaser of goods and services. In 2019, total government expenditures were $428.3 billion, and the federal "Investing in Canada Plan" committed $180 billion to infrastructure improvements.
- **As a Competitor:** Government competes with private business through **Crown corporations** at both the federal and provincial levels, such as Canada Post and Hydro-Québec.
- **As a Regulator:** Government regulates business activity to protect competition, consumers, the environment, and to achieve social goals. The **Competition Act** is a key piece of legislation that prohibits anti-competitive practices like price-fixing. Recent changes to the act increased fines for misleading marketing from $100,000 to $10 million.
- **As a Taxation Agent:** Governments levy taxes to fund services and programs. These include **revenue taxes** (income tax), **regressive taxes** (sales tax), and **restrictive taxes** (on tobacco and alcohol).
- **As a Provider of Incentives:** Governments offer programs to stimulate economic development, totaling over $29 billion annually from federal and major provincial governments. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the federal government provided over $95 billion in loans and tax deferrals in the first month, along with a 75% wage subsidy for qualifying companies.
- **As a Provider of Essential Services:** Governments supply highways, postal service, police and fire departments, and utilities, creating a stable environment for business activity.

### How Business Influences Government

- **Lobbyists:** A **lobbyist** is a person hired by a company or industry to represent its interests with government officials. The federal Lobbying Act requires lobbyists to register and adhere to rules of transparency.
- **Trade Associations:** These organizations promote the interests of a particular industry and can act as a collective lobby. They also provide training, host trade shows, and publish industry newsletters.

## Social Responsibility: The B Corporation Model

The rise of the **B Corporation (B Corp)** reflects a growing movement to blend profit with social and environmental goals. B Corps are for-profit companies that voluntarily meet high standards of social and environmental performance, transparency, and accountability, as graded by the not-for-profit B Lab.

An example is **EcoZoom**, a B Corp that manufactures and sells low-emission, energy-efficient cookstoves. While sold in developed nations for camping, its primary market is industrializing countries like Rwanda. The stoves reduce household air pollution—a major cause of illness—and cut fuel use by up to 30%, freeing up family income and time.

## Core Principles of the Canadian Market Economy

### The Laws of Demand and Supply

The Canadian market economy operates on the principles of demand and supply.

- **Demand:** The willingness and ability of buyers to purchase a product. The **law of demand** states that as price drops, demand increases.
- **Supply:** The willingness and ability of producers to offer a product for sale. The **law of supply** states that as price rises, producers will supply more.
- **Equilibrium Price:** The intersection of the demand and supply curves determines the **market price** or **equilibrium price**, where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied.
- **Surplus and Shortage:** A **surplus** occurs when supply exceeds demand, leading to wasted resources. A **shortage** occurs when demand exceeds supply, leading to lost potential profits and dissatisfied customers.

The pandemic provided stark examples: demand for Clorox sanitizing products surged, while demand for oil plummeted to the point where it briefly sold at a negative price (-$4.47 per barrel on April 20, 2020) because there was nowhere to store the oversupply.

### Private Enterprise and Competition

Canada's system is based on **private enterprise**, which requires four elements: private property rights, freedom of choice, the pursuit of profits, and competition. Competition forces businesses to operate efficiently, making products better or cheaper.

#### The Four Degrees of Competition

|   |   |   |   |   |
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Characteristic|Perfect Competition|Monopolistic Competition|Oligopoly|Monopoly|
|**Number of Competitors**|Many|Many|Few|None|
|**Similarity of Goods**|Identical|Similar but differentiated|Can be similar or different|No direct competition|
|**Ease of Entry**|Relatively easy|Fairly easy|Difficult|Regulated by government|
|**Control Over Price**|None|Some|Some|Considerable|
|**Example**|Canadian agriculture (wheat)|Clothing stores, restaurants|Game consoles (Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo), steel industry|Provincial electrical utilities, Canada Post (for letters)|

A notable feature in Canada is **supply management**, a system for commodities like dairy, eggs, and poultry. It establishes domestic production quotas and uses high tariffs (e.g., 299% on butter) to block foreign competition, giving it characteristics of a monopoly. To compensate for lost protection under new trade deals, the Canadian government scheduled cash payments for dairy farmers totaling over $1.4 billion between 2020 and 2023.
