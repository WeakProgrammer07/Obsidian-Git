# The Principles of Business Enterprise Management

## Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of the core principles and processes of managing a business enterprise. Effective management is presented as a multifaceted discipline blending science and art, revolving around four key activities: **planning, organizing, leading, and controlling**. Managers operate at distinct levels—top, middle, and first-line—and across various functional areas, requiring a versatile skill set to succeed.

The five essential management skills identified are **technical, human relations, conceptual, time management, and decision-making**. The decision-making process is detailed as a rational, step-by-step sequence, but is also influenced by behavioral factors like intuition and organizational politics.

Strategic management is crucial for aligning an organization with its external environment. This begins with setting clear, **SMART goals** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Results-oriented, Time-framed) derived from the company's vision and mission. The primary tool for strategy formulation is the **SWOT analysis**, which assesses internal Strengths and Weaknesses against external Opportunities and Threats.

Finally, modern management must prepare for uncertainty through **contingency planning** and **crisis management**. The organization's identity is defined by its **corporate culture**—the shared values, stories, and norms that guide behavior. This culture must be actively communicated and managed to ensure alignment with organizational goals. The case of Canadian Tire Corporation under CEO Greg Hicks during the COVID-19 pandemic serves as a practical illustration of these principles in action, demonstrating how decisive leadership, strategic foresight, and a robust supply chain enabled the company to thrive amidst unprecedented crisis.

## The Core Functions of Management

Management is defined as the process of planning, organizing, leading, and controlling an enterprise’s financial, physical, human, and information resources to achieve its goals. These four functions are interrelated, and a manager is likely to engage in all of them during a typical business day. A key distinction exists between management efficiency (achieving the greatest output with a given input; "doing things right") and management effectiveness (achieving set organizational goals; "doing the right things").

- **Planning:** This is the process of determining the firm’s goals and developing a strategy to achieve them. It involves five steps:
    1. Establishing goals for the organization.
    2. Identifying any gap between the desired and actual position.
    3. Developing plans to achieve the desired objectives.
    4. Implementing the chosen plans.
    5. Assessing the effectiveness of the plan and making adjustments.
- **Organizing:** This function involves mobilizing and arranging the resources and jobs required to complete a task. It helps everyone in the organization understand their roles and reporting relationships, often visualized through organization charts.
- **Leading (or Directing):** This involves the interactions between managers and their subordinates to meet the firm's objectives. It goes beyond giving orders to include motivating employees, fostering respect and trust, and uniting them to work in the best interests of the company.
- **Controlling:** This is the process of monitoring a firm's performance to ensure it is meeting its goals. It involves establishing standards, measuring actual performance against those standards, and taking corrective action if performance varies significantly.

## The Managerial Hierarchy

Managers can be categorized by their level of responsibility within the organization and by their specific area of management.

### Levels of Management

Most organizations have three basic levels of management, each with distinct responsibilities.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Level|Examples|Responsibilities|
|**Top Managers**|President, CEO, COO, CFO, Vice-President|Responsible for the firm's overall performance and effectiveness. They set general policies, formulate long-range strategies, approve significant decisions, and represent the company externally.|
|**Middle Managers**|Plant Manager, Operations Manager, Division Manager|Responsible for implementing the strategies, policies, and decisions made by top managers.|
|**First-Line Managers**|Supervisor, Office Manager, Group Leader, Project Manager|Responsible for supervising the work of the employees who report to them, ensuring they are properly trained and understand company policies.|

### Areas of Management

Within these levels, managers operate in various functional areas:

- **Human Resource Managers:** Responsible for hiring, training, performance evaluation, compensation, and labor relations.
- **Operations Managers:** Responsible for the systems that create goods and services, including production, inventory, and quality control.
- **Information Managers:** Responsible for designing and implementing systems to gather, process, and disseminate information.
- **Marketing Managers:** Responsible for the development, pricing, promotion, and distribution of products and services to buyers.
- **Financial Managers:** Responsible for planning and overseeing the firm's financial resources.
- **Other Managers:** Specialized roles such as Research and Development (R&D), Public Relations, or Diversity and Inclusion managers.

## Essential Management Skills and Roles

To be successful, managers must possess a diverse set of skills and be adept at playing various roles within the organization.

### Management Roles

According to research by Henry Mintzberg, a manager's job can be described through ten specific roles that fall into three general categories:

1. **Interpersonal Roles:** Arise from formal authority and include being a figurehead, a leader, and a liaison.
2. **Informational Roles:** Stem from the interpersonal roles and involve being a monitor, a disseminator, and a spokesperson.
3. **Decision-Making Roles:** Allow the manager to take action and include being an entrepreneur, a disturbance handler, a resource allocator, and a negotiator.

### The Five Basic Management Skills

1. **Technical Skills:** The ability to perform specialized tasks (e.g., accounting, programming). These skills are most critical for first-line managers who directly supervise employees performing these tasks.
2. **Human Relations Skills:** The ability to understand, lead, motivate, and communicate with other people. These are crucial at all management levels, as managers act as "bridges" between their superiors, subordinates, and peers. Poor people skills are cited as the top reason for managerial failure.
3. **Conceptual Skills:** The ability to think in the abstract, diagnose complex situations, and see beyond the present. These skills are most vital for top managers who must formulate long-term strategies.
4. **Time Management Skills:** The productive use of time. Managers must learn to overcome the four leading causes of wasted time: paperwork, telephone interruptions, unproductive meetings, and non-essential email/SMS.
5. **Decision-Making Skills:** The ability to define problems or opportunities and select the best course of action. This involves a rational process but is also influenced by behavioral factors.

#### The Rational Decision-Making Process

The formal process for making logical decisions includes six steps:

1. **Recognizing and Defining the Decision Situation:** Identifying a problem or an opportunity that requires a decision.
2. **Identifying Alternatives:** Generating a list of possible courses of action.
3. **Evaluating Alternatives:** Assessing each alternative for its feasibility, satisfactoriness, and potential consequences.
4. **Selecting the Best Alternative:** Choosing the alternative that best fits the manager's situation.
5. **Implementing the Chosen Alternative:** Putting the selected course of action into the organizational system.
6. **Following Up and Evaluating the Results:** Ascertaining the extent to which the chosen alternative has worked.

#### Behavioral Aspects of Decision Making

Managerial decisions are often influenced by non-logical factors:

- **Organizational Politics:** Actions people take to get what they want, which may or may not benefit the organization.
- **Intuition:** Making a decision based on a "hunch" or feeling, usually derived from years of experience.
- **Escalation of Commitment:** Remaining committed to a bad decision despite clear evidence that it was a mistake.
- **Risk Propensity:** The degree to which a manager is willing to gamble when making decisions.

## Strategic Management: Aligning the Organization with its Environment

Strategic management is the process of effectively aligning an organization with its external environment, starting with the establishment of clear goals and the formulation of a broad strategy to achieve them.

### Goal Setting

Goals are performance targets that provide direction, help allocate resources, define corporate culture, and serve as a benchmark for assessing performance.

- **Vision and Mission:** An organization's **vision** (or purpose) indicates why it exists and what it wants to be. Its **mission statement** outlines how it will achieve its purpose.
- **Types of Goals:** Goals are set across different time horizons:
    - **Long-Term Goals:** Typically five years or more.
    - **Intermediate Goals:** One to five years.
    - **Short-Term Goals:** One year or less.
- **SMART Goals:** Research shows that performance is highest when goals are **S**pecific, **M**easurable, **A**chievable, **R**esults-oriented, and **T**ime-framed.

### Strategy Formulation

Strategy formulation is a three-step process for creating a broad program to meet organizational goals.

1. **Set Strategic Goals:** These are long-term goals derived directly from the firm's mission statement.
2. **Analyze the Organization and its Environment (SWOT Analysis):** This involves identifying internal organizational **S**trengths and **W**eaknesses, as well as external environmental **O**pportunities and **T**hreats.
3. **Match the Organization and its Environment:** This final step involves leveraging strengths to capitalize on opportunities, counteracting threats, and shielding weaknesses.

### Levels of Strategy

A firm’s overall strategy is hierarchical, comprising three distinct levels:

- **Corporate-Level Strategy:** Identifies which businesses a company will be in and how they relate. Strategies include:
    - **Concentration:** Focusing on one product or product line.
    - **Growth:** Expanding sales, geographic reach, or product offerings.
    - **Integration:** Acquiring competitors (horizontal) or controlling inputs/distribution channels (vertical).
    - **Diversification:** Entering new, related or unrelated, markets.
    - **Investment Reduction:** Reducing activity (retrenchment) or selling off businesses (divestment).
- **Business-Level (Competitive) Strategy:** Identifies how a business will compete in its chosen market. Strategies include:
    - **Cost Leadership:** Becoming the lowest-cost producer (e.g., Walmart).
    - **Differentiation:** Being unique along a dimension valued by buyers (e.g., Volvo emphasizes safety).
    - **Focus:** Serving a specific market niche better than competitors (e.g., lululemon and the yoga market).
- **Functional Strategy:** Identifies the basic course of action for each department (e.g., marketing, finance) to support the business-level strategy.

## Planning for the Unforeseen: Contingency and Crisis Management

Because business environments are unpredictable, effective management includes planning for unexpected events.

- **Contingency Planning:** The process of identifying in advance potential changes that could affect a business (e.g., a snowstorm, a hurricane) and developing a plan to respond. This allows managers to assess costs and benefits ahead of time and react effectively when problems arise.
- **Crisis Management:** The process of dealing with an emergency that demands an immediate response. Crises can be self-inflicted (e.g., the Boeing 737 Max mismanagement) or external (e.g., data breaches, pandemics). A crisis management plan outlines who is in charge and how the organization will respond. Recent examples of crises include data breaches at Microsoft, Desjardins Group, and Equifax, and the discovery of tainted meat at a Maple Leaf Foods plant.

## Corporate Culture: The Organizational Identity

Every company has a unique identity known as its corporate culture, defined as the shared experiences, stories, beliefs, and norms that characterize it—informally, "the way we do things around here."

- **Importance of Culture:** A strong corporate culture guides employees toward the same goals, helps newcomers learn accepted behaviors, and can be a significant factor in organizational success. Examples include Google's "culture of 'yes'" to encourage innovation and Costco's focus on customer service and employee satisfaction.
- **Communicating and Managing Culture:** Managers must have a clear understanding of the culture and work to transmit it throughout the organization via training, mission statements, and by rewarding those who uphold it.
- **Changing Culture:** Changing an organization's culture can be extremely difficult. The Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP), for instance, has struggled for over a decade to reform what has been described as a "horribly broken" and "toxic" culture of harassment and discrimination.

## Case Study in Practice: Canadian Tire Corporation's Pandemic Response

The experience of Canadian Tire Corporation (CTC) when Greg Hicks became CEO on March 12, 2020—one day after COVID-19 was declared a global pandemic—provides a powerful real-world example of management principles in action.

- **Strategic Foresight (Planning):** Hicks' prior work as president of Canadian Tire Retail (CTR) had pushed the company to improve its digital and e-commerce strategy. These pre-pandemic investments in technology were key to navigating the crisis.
- **Crisis Management & Decisive Leadership:** Facing store closures and an overnight explosion in e-commerce orders (from 4,000 to 120,000 daily), Hicks and his team made quick, decisive moves. This included redirecting 200 containers of bicycles from closed Sport Chek locations to open Canadian Tire stores.
- **Leveraging Strengths (SWOT):** CTC's 100-year-old supply chain network was a major organizational strength, enabling it to pivot quickly and organize resources to meet shifting consumer demands, such as for exercise equipment and paint products for homebound customers.
- **Performance & Control:** Despite the turmoil, CTC's performance was strong. Total sales grew 11.8% in Q3 2020, while CTR's sales grew 18.6% in Q3 and 25.7% in Q4 2020. Total e-commerce sales were up 132% in Q3 2020. This success demonstrates the effective implementation and control of its crisis response strategy.
