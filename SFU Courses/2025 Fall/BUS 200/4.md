# Briefing on Entrepreneurship and New Venture Creation in Canada

## Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive synthesis of entrepreneurship, small business, and new venture creation within the Canadian context. Small businesses, defined as owner-managed enterprises with fewer than 100 employees, represent 97.9% of all businesses in Canada and contribute approximately 30% of the nation's GDP. They are a primary driver of job creation, accounting for 35.8% of all private-sector jobs between 2014 and 2019.

The entrepreneurial process is a journey centered on three key elements: the entrepreneur, the opportunity, and the resources. Successful entrepreneurs exhibit characteristics such as resourcefulness, a desire for independence, and the ability to manage uncertainty and risk. The process involves generating and screening ideas to identify viable opportunities, followed by accessing necessary resources, often through "bootstrapping." Financing is critical, with options divided into equity (personal savings, private investors, venture capital) and debt (financial institutions, trade credit). A robust support ecosystem exists, including the Business Development Bank of Canada (BDC), business incubators, and crowdfunding platforms.

There are three primary pathways to business ownership: starting a new venture from scratch, buying an existing business, and purchasing a franchise. While starting from scratch offers maximum control, buying an existing business provides a proven track record. Franchising offers brand recognition and management support but involves significant costs and contractual constraints.

Success in small business is typically attributed to hard work, market demand, managerial competence, and luck. Conversely, failure often stems from managerial incompetence, neglect, weak control systems, and insufficient capital. Entrepreneurs must also select an appropriate legal structure, with four main options available: the sole proprietorship (simple, but with unlimited liability), the partnership (shared resources, but joint unlimited liability), the corporation (limited liability, but complex and subject to double taxation), and the cooperative (democratic control, but challenges in attracting equity). The business landscape is also being reshaped by emerging trends like the "gig economy," which involves a growing number of Canadians participating in non-traditional, freelance, or contract-based work.

## The Landscape of Canadian Entrepreneurship

The Canadian business environment is fundamentally shaped by the interplay of small businesses, new ventures, and the spirit of entrepreneurship. While closely linked, these concepts are distinct.

- **Small Business:** An independently owned and managed business with fewer than 100 employees that does not dominate its market. These businesses are the backbone of the economy, with 97.9% of all Canadian businesses falling into this category. The government uses varied definitions based on employee count (fewer than 100 for goods-producing, fewer than 50 for service-producing) for statistical purposes.
- **New Venture:** A recently formed commercial organization, operational within the last 12 months, that provides goods and/or services for sale.
- **Entrepreneurship:** The process of identifying an opportunity in the marketplace and accessing the resources needed to capitalize on it. Entrepreneurs are individuals who recognize and seize these opportunities, accepting both the risks and rewards.

A prime example of sustained entrepreneurial success is the **Jim Pattison Group**. Founded in 1961, it has grown into a private empire with $10.9 billion in annual sales and 48,000 employees across diverse sectors. Notably, founder Jim Pattison has consistently rejected taking the company public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO), preferring the strategic freedom from short-term market pressures that private ownership affords. This demonstrates that there is no single path to building a large, successful enterprise.

## The Economic Impact of Small and New Businesses

Small and new businesses are critical drivers of the Canadian economy, a role that has gained significant recognition over the last three decades. Their contributions are substantial in terms of job creation, innovation, and economic output.

### Key Economic Contributions

- **GDP Contribution:** Small businesses have contributed approximately 30% of Canada's GDP over the past decade. British Columbia has the highest rate of contribution at 33%.
- **Business Concentration:** Prince Edward Island has the highest concentration of businesses per 1,000 people (50.8), followed by Alberta (48.3).
- **Employment:** In 2020, there were over 12.3 million employees in Canada's private sector. Small businesses employ at least half the workforce in all industries and over 80% in sectors like agriculture, construction, and accommodation/food services.
- **Job Creation:** New firms are the primary source of job creation. From 2014 to 2019, small businesses created 35.8% of all jobs in the private sector.
- **Innovation:** New ventures are responsible for the majority of new products and services introduced to the market.

### Diversity in Entrepreneurship

The entrepreneurial landscape is becoming increasingly diverse, with dedicated programs and a growing presence of women, Indigenous, and Black entrepreneurs.

- **Women Entrepreneurs:** Women now account for half of all new businesses formed, though they lead only 14.8% of small and medium-sized businesses that export. Key motivations for women starting businesses include gaining control over their schedule (46%), seizing a market opportunity (24%), and frustration with the "glass ceiling" in large companies (23%).
- **Indigenous and Black Entrepreneurs:** Organizations like the Business Development Bank of Canada (BDC) offer specific loan programs for Indigenous entrepreneurs. Ryerson University's Black Innovation Fellowship aims to address systemic challenges by providing seed money and role models. The success of **Manitobah Mukluks**, a Métis-owned company with revenues in the $50 million range, exemplifies the power of authentic, culturally-rooted entrepreneurship. The company, which has a workforce that is approximately 70% Indigenous, promotes its heritage while achieving significant growth and global brand recognition.

## The Entrepreneurial Process

The creation of a new venture is a dynamic process influenced by social, economic, and technological factors. It revolves around the interaction of three key elements: the entrepreneur, the opportunity, and the necessary resources.

### 1. The Entrepreneur: Characteristics and Goals

Successful entrepreneurs share common traits, regardless of their ultimate goals. These include resourcefulness, a desire for independence, a strong focus on customer relations, and the ability to deal with uncertainty and risk.

- **Entrepreneurial Goals:** Motivations vary. Some seek independence and financial security for their families, while others aim for massive growth and expansion, like Howard Schultz did with Starbucks.
- **Intrapreneurs:** These are individuals who exhibit entrepreneurial characteristics within an existing large organization, creating new products or processes. A study by the Wharton School of Business found that of the top 30 inventions in the last three decades, 22 were developed by intrapreneurs.

### 2. Identifying Opportunities

This stage involves generating, screening, and developing business ideas.

- **Idea Generation:** Most ideas (45% to 85%) originate from an individual's work experience, providing deep familiarity with a product, market, and customer needs. Other sources include personal hobbies (16%) and chance happenings (11%).
- **Screening Ideas:** To move from an idea to a viable opportunity, it must be carefully screened against key criteria:
    - **Creates or Adds Value:** Solves a significant problem or meets a customer need in a new way.
    - **Provides a Sustainable Competitive Advantage:** Offers something that customers perceive as better than competitors' offerings.
    - **Is Marketable and Financially Viable:** Has sufficient customer demand to generate profits, supported by sales forecasts and financial projections.
    - **Has Low Exit Costs:** Can be shut down without a significant loss of time, money, or reputation if it proves unviable.
- **Developing the Opportunity:** This requires creating a business concept and an entry strategy (e.g., introducing a new product, customizing an existing one, or franchising). A formal **business plan** is often essential, especially when seeking investment.

### 3. Accessing Resources

Entrepreneurs typically acquire resources through **bootstrapping**, which means "doing more with less" by utilizing other people's resources and minimizing expenses.

## Pathways to Business Ownership

Entrepreneurs can become business owners through three main strategies, each with distinct advantages and challenges.

### 1. Starting from Scratch

This path involves executing the full entrepreneurial process, from idea generation to launch. It offers the highest degree of control and creative freedom but also carries the most risk.

### 2. Buying an Existing Business

This strategy increases the chances of success, as the business has a proven ability to attract customers, established relationships with stakeholders, and a clear financial track record. However, potential downsides include inheriting a poor reputation, a bad location, or uncertainty about the business's true financial health.

- **Taking Over a Family Business:** This presents unique opportunities, such as access to resources and a loyal employee base, but also challenges like succession conflicts and managing family disagreements. **Kal Tire**, started in 1953 and now a major company with over 250 locations, is a positive example of a thriving multi-generational family business.

### 3. Buying a Franchise

Franchising accounts for 40% of retail sales in Canada, with over 76,000 establishments employing 1.9 million people. In this model, a franchisee buys the right to sell the product or service of a franchiser.

## Factors of Success and Failure

Analysis of small business performance reveals common factors that contribute to both success and failure. On average, 85% of new businesses survive one year, 70% survive three years, and 51% survive five years.

### Reasons for Success

1. **Hard Work, Drive, and Dedication:** A willingness to commit the time and effort necessary, especially in the early years.
2. **Market Demand:** Providing a product or service that meets a clear and sufficient market need.
3. **Managerial Competence:** Possessing a solid understanding of business management principles, acquired through training or experience.
4. **Luck:** Being in the right place at the right time, though this cannot be relied upon.

### Reasons for Failure

1. **Managerial Incompetence or Inexperience:** Overestimating one's own skills and lacking a grasp of basic business decision-making.
2. **Neglect:** Trying to run a business as a side project without the overwhelming time commitment it requires.
3. **Weak Control Systems:** A lack of effective systems to monitor performance and signal potential trouble before it becomes critical.
4. **Insufficient Capital:** Overly optimistic profit forecasts leading to a lack of funds to cover expenses during the initial months or years of operation.

## Legal Forms of Business Organization

Choosing the right legal structure is a foundational decision for any entrepreneur. Each form offers a different balance of liability, complexity, and tax implications.

## Emerging Trends: The Rise of the Gig Economy

The Canadian workforce is undergoing a significant transformation with the growth of the "gig economy," characterized by less structured, non-traditional work environments.

- **Definition:** The gig economy encompasses freelancers, contractors, and individuals engaged in temporary, often technology-enabled, work. Examples include food delivery drivers (Uber Eats), property renters (Airbnb), driving services (Uber), and freelance web designers.
- **Prevalence:** Approximately 17% of the Canadian workforce participates in the gig economy. This includes nearly half of all millennials and a quarter of individuals over age 55. Participation spans all income levels, with 31% of households earning over $150,000 having engaged in gig work.
- **Motivations:** Reasons for participating include earning extra income, lack of full-time job opportunities, managing rising living costs, and seeking greater work-life balance and flexibility.
- **Implications:** This shift challenges traditional employment models. As more workers opt for the independence of gig work, large businesses may face difficulties in recruiting and retaining employees for long-term roles, potentially leading to a greater reliance on the service and gig sectors for overall economic health.
