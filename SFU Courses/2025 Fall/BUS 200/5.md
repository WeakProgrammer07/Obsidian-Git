# Briefing Document: Business Ethics and Corporate Social Responsibility

## Executive Summary

This document synthesizes the core principles of business ethics and Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR). Ethics are defined as beliefs about right and wrong, shaped by individual morals and social norms, which guide behavior in the workplace. Unethical conduct stems from a combination of pressure, opportunity, and rationalization, and can be mitigated through demonstrated commitment from top management, written codes of ethics, and comprehensive employee training. Corporate Social Responsibility extends ethical principles to an organization's obligations to its key stakeholders—customers, employees, investors, suppliers, and communities—as well as its impact on the natural environment. Businesses approach CSR across a spectrum, from an obstructionist stance of minimal compliance to a proactive stance of seeking opportunities to contribute positively. Effective CSR management requires formal activities like strategic planning and social audits, alongside informal cultural elements that encourage ethical leadership and protect whistle-blowers. Key modern challenges include navigating global variations in business practices, addressing ethical dilemmas posed by new technology, and tackling significant environmental issues such as pollution, climate change, and the deceptive practice of "greenwashing."

## 1. Foundations of Business Ethics

### Defining Ethics in the Workplace

Ethics are the beliefs individuals hold about what is right and wrong or good and bad. These beliefs are shaped by personal values, morals, and the social context.

- **Ethical vs. Unethical Behaviour:** Ethical behaviour conforms to individual beliefs and social norms about what is right, while unethical behaviour is defined by those same beliefs and norms as wrong.
- **Business Ethics:** This term refers specifically to the ethical or unethical behaviours of a business's managers or employees.
- **Individual Ethics Formation:** An individual's ethical standards begin to form in childhood based on perceptions of adults and are later influenced by peers, media, and personal values (e.g., prioritizing financial gain versus family).
- **Unethical vs. Illegal Behaviour:** A critical distinction exists between unethical and illegal acts. Behaviours can be:
    - **Ethical and Legal:** Providing high-quality products.
    - **Ethical and Illegal:** Breaking unjust laws in a totalitarian regime for humanitarian reasons.
    - **Unethical and Legal:** Paying very low wages in a foreign country where it is permitted.
    - **Unethical and Illegal:** Falsifying financial records ("cooking the books").
- **Cultural Variations:** Ethical perceptions vary significantly across cultures. For example, the Brazilian philosophy of _jeitinho_ involves using personal connections or bending rules to accomplish a task, a practice that might be viewed differently in other cultural contexts.

### Managerial Ethics: Standards in Practice

Managerial ethics are the standards of behaviour that guide individual managers in their work. These standards apply to three main areas of professional conduct.

**Behaviour Toward Employees:**

- **Hiring and Firing:** Decisions should be based on ability. Discrimination against ethnic minorities is both unethical and illegal in Canada. Hiring a less-qualified friend or relative may be legal but is often considered unethical.
- **Wages and Working Conditions:** Paying an employee less than they deserve because they cannot afford to quit is an example of an ethically debatable business practice.
- **Privacy:** In Canada, the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) requires organizations to obtain consent before collecting or using personal information.
- **Employee Dissent:** Employees may protest company policies they find unethical. In 2020, 400 Facebook employees walked off the job to protest the company's handling of aggressive posts from former president Donald Trump.

**Behaviour Toward the Organization:**

- **Honesty:** Common issues include employees stealing supplies or padding expense accounts.
- **Conflict of Interest:** This occurs when an activity benefits an employee at the expense of their employer. An example is a buyer for a department store accepting a free vacation from a supplier, which may obligate them to place larger orders.

**Behaviour Toward Other Economic Agents:**

- This category includes relationships with customers, competitors, suppliers, dealers, unions, and shareholders.
- **Examples of Ethical Disputes:**
    - **Ultimatums:** Caterpillar Inc. closed its London, Ontario plant after demanding a 50% wage cut that the union refused.
    - **Competitor Information:** Using confidential information about a competitor obtained from a disgruntled former employee is ethically ambiguous.
    - **Bribery:** While considered normal in some countries, bribery is unethical and illegal in Canada. High-profile cases have involved Samsung, SNC-Lavalin (fined $280 million for fraud related to bribes in Libya), and Bombardier.
    - **Perception of Corruption:** A global survey found that while 40% of managers worldwide felt corruption was widespread, only 20% of Canadian managers felt this way about Canada. The OECD, however, has expressed concern about loopholes in Canada's bribery laws.

### A Framework for Ethical Assessment

A three-step model can be used to systematically apply ethical judgments to business situations:

1. **Gather the relevant factual information.**
2. **Determine the most appropriate moral values.**
3. **Make an ethical judgment based on the rightness or wrongness of the activity.**

To analyze the facts (Step 2), four common ethical norms are used:

- **Utility:** Does the act optimize what is best for those affected by it?
- **Rights:** Does it respect the rights of the individuals involved?
- **Justice:** Is it consistent with what is regarded as fair?
- **Caring:** Is it consistent with people’s responsibilities to each other?

## 2. Cultivating an Ethical Organization

To promote ethical behaviour, organizations must first understand its root causes and then implement structured strategies to encourage it.

### Causes of Unethical Behavior

Three general factors contribute to unethical actions in the workplace:

1. **Pressure:** An employee has a problem that cannot be solved through legitimate means.
2. **Opportunity:** The employee uses their position within the organization to secretly solve the problem.
3. **Rationalization:** The employee sees themselves as an ethical person caught in an unfortunate situation.

### Strategies for Promoting Ethical Conduct

Organizations can implement three key strategies to foster an ethical environment.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Strategy|Description|Key Examples & Details|
|**Top Management Commitment**|Senior leadership must demonstrate a serious and public commitment to high ethical standards to set the "tone at the top."|Mountain Equipment Company's commitment to _ethical sourcing_ ensures good working conditions in its suppliers' factories. Google's core principle is "Don't Be Evil," which remains unchanging even as business strategies adapt.|
|**Written Codes of Ethics**|Formally acknowledges a company's intent to do business ethically, increasing public confidence and helping managers respond to issues.|The Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation (CDIC) requires all deposit-taking institutions to have a code of conduct. For codes to be effective, they must be supported by a control system and consistent enforcement, unlike the apparent failures at Boeing during the 737 MAX development.|
|**Ethics Training**|Programs designed to educate employees about ethical decision-making, current laws, and strategies for resisting pressure to act unethically.|Can involve sensitizing employees to issues like plagiarism and cheating. Some companies use seminars taught by former executives who have served prison time for ethical misdeeds. Consultant Mary Gentile argues training should focus on how to act on one's values under pressure.|

## 3. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): A Stakeholder-Centric Approach

### Defining CSR and Its Core Debate

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) refers to how a business balances its commitments to the individuals and groups in its external environment. These individuals and groups are known as **organizational stakeholders**.

- **The CSR Debate:**
    - **Managerial Capitalism:** This view holds that a company's only responsibility is to maximize profits for its shareholders, as long as it operates within the law.
    - **Stakeholder View:** This opposing view argues that companies have a responsibility to a broad range of stakeholders, including customers, employees, investors, suppliers, and local communities. This perspective is supported by the belief that businesses are corporate citizens who should help improve society.
- **B Corps:** Business firms can become certified as a "B Corp" by the non-profit B Lab for meeting rigorous standards of inclusion, sustainability, equity, and diversity. There are approximately 230 B Corps in Canada, including Fairware, Oliberté, Beau's brewery, and Danone Canada.
- **Fair-Trade Movement:** A movement designed to help workers in developing countries receive fair payment for their work and to enforce child labor laws. Organizations like Fairtrade International certify producers, allowing them to use the FAIRTRADE mark on products.

### The Stakeholder Model: Key Areas of Responsibility

Socially responsible companies concentrate on their commitments to five primary stakeholders and the natural environment.

**Responsibility Toward Customers:**

- **Consumer Rights:** The consumerism movement advocates for consumer rights, including:
    1. The right to safe products (highlighted by the Maple Leaf Foods listeria case, which caused 20 deaths).
    2. The right to be informed about products.
    3. The right to be heard (e.g., Procter & Gamble's toll-free numbers).
    4. The right to choose what they buy.
    5. The right to be educated about purchases.
    6. The right to courteous service.
- **Unfair Pricing:** Collusion among companies to "fix" prices is illegal. Loblaw Companies Limited admitted to participating in a bread price-fixing scheme from 2001–2016. Penalties in Canada for price-fixing include up to 14 years in prison and a $25 million fine.
- **Ethics in Advertising:**
    - **Truth in Advertising:** Claims must be demonstrably true. Sony once invented a fake movie critic to give its films rave reviews.
    - **Stealth Advertising:** Paying individuals to promote products to others without disclosing the relationship.
    - **Morally Objectionable Advertising:** Portrayals that offend customers' sense of decency.
    - **Counterfeit Brands:** A major issue affecting numerous product lines. The U.S. government has cited Pacific Mall in Markham, Ontario, for pervasive sales of counterfeit goods.

**Responsibility Toward Employees:**

- **Fair Practices:** Socially responsible firms hire and promote without regard to race, sex, or other irrelevant factors; provide a safe and non-bullying workplace; do not tolerate sexual harassment; and promote work-life balance.
- **Privacy vs. Monitoring:** There is controversy over employee monitoring, including drug testing and tracking computer and social media activity. Amazon uses algorithms to monitor the "pick-rate" of warehouse workers.
- **Whistle-Blowers:** An employee who publicizes a company's unethical, illegal, or socially irresponsible actions. Whistle-blowers often face demotion or termination. Protections exist, such as a hotline opened by the Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC). During the COVID-19 pandemic, whistle-blowers exposed shortages of PPE in long-term care facilities.

**Responsibility Toward Investors:**

- **Improper Financial Management:** Includes executives paying themselves outlandish salaries and bonuses. Bombardier faced public outrage in 2017 for giving five top executives a nearly 50% pay increase amid financial struggles.
- **Misrepresentation of Finances:** Falsifying financial status, often in the form of Ponzi schemes. Examples include the collapse of Vancouver-based cryptocurrency exchange QuadrigaCX, which lost $215 million, and the infamous Bernie Madoff fraud.
- **Cheque Kiting:** Illegally using money from a second bank account while a cheque from the first account is still in transit.
- **Insider Trading:** Using confidential information to profit from the purchase or sale of stock. Cases include executives at Grande Cache Coal Corporation and Galleon Group co-founder Raj Rajaratnam.

**Responsibility Toward Suppliers and Communities:**

- **Suppliers:** Socially responsible companies foster mutually beneficial partnerships, keeping suppliers informed and negotiating fair terms. Toyota and Amazon are noted for excellent supplier relationships.
- **Local & International Communities:** Businesses demonstrate responsibility through charitable donations and volunteerism. Examples include Canada Goose providing free materials to Inuit sewers and TELUS's "Celebration of Giving" program.

## 4. Environmental Responsibility

Controlling pollution—the injection of harmful substances into the environment—is a significant component of CSR.

### Addressing Pollution

- **Air Pollution:** Caused by emissions from automobiles and manufacturing. Canada's air quality has improved over the past 30 years, with significant declines in pollutants like sulphur dioxide (-92%) and carbon monoxide (-90%). However, Health Canada estimates air pollution still causes 15,300 premature deaths annually, with an economic cost of $120 billion.
- **Water Pollution:** Results from businesses and municipalities dumping waste into waterways. Pollution from ships is also a significant problem.
- **Land Pollution:** Includes toxic waste from manufacturing and challenges with solid waste disposal. This has spurred the recycling industry. Keurig Canada now makes its coffee pods from recyclable material.

### Key Environmental Challenges and Initiatives

- **Climate Change & The Paris Agreement:** An agreement signed by 196 countries to reduce carbon dioxide emissions and limit the rise in global temperatures to no more than 2 degrees Celsius.
- **Carbon Tax:** A controversial tax intended to create a financial incentive for companies to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. In 2021, the Canadian federal carbon price stood at $40/tonne, scheduled to rise to $170/tonne by 2030. Critics argue it slows economic growth, while supporters see it as necessary.
- **Recycling and Biomass:** Recycling re-converts waste into useful products. Biomass involves recycling plant and animal waste (e.g., sawdust, manure) to produce energy.
- **Fracking:** The process of injecting water and chemicals into underground rock to extract oil. While it has increased oil supply and lowered prices, environmentalists argue it pollutes water sources and causes earthquakes.

### The Consumer Role and the Problem of "Greenwashing"

- **Consumer Action and Skepticism:** Consumers can reduce pollution through recycling and other actions, but motivation varies. A survey showed that while one-third of Canadians often purchase green products, 78% are unwilling to pay the higher prices.
- **Greenwashing:** The practice of making misleading claims about a product's environmental benefits. A TerraChoice study found misleading green claims on 95.6% of products examined, with 100% of toy manufacturers guilty of the practice. The proliferation of 455 different eco-labels across 199 countries further confuses consumers.

## 5. Implementing and Managing Social Responsibility

Organizations adopt different approaches to CSR and can manage their programs through both formal and informal means.

### A Spectrum of CSR Approaches

Organizations typically adopt one of four stances on social responsibility, ranging from lowest to highest commitment.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Stance|Description|Example|
|**Obstructionist**|Do as little as possible to solve social or environmental problems; may deny or cover up wrongdoing.|Volkswagen's attempt to falsify emissions test readings for its diesel engines.|
|**Defensive**|Do everything that is legally required, but nothing more.|Tobacco companies that follow advertising laws to the letter but use more aggressive marketing in less-regulated countries.|
|**Accommodative**|Meet legal and ethical requirements and will go further if asked.|A company that donates to a local sports team only after being solicited for the donation.|
|**Proactive**|Actively seek opportunities to contribute to society; view themselves as good corporate citizens.|Companies that set up foundations to provide financial support for social programs, such as the Calgary Foundation.|

### Formal and Informal Management of CSR Programs

- **Formal Activities:**
    - **Top Management Support:** Leadership must make CSR a factor in strategic planning.
    - **Program Direction:** A specific executive is often appointed to oversee the social agenda.
    - **Social Audit:** A systematic analysis of how a firm is using funds earmarked for social responsibility goals and how effective those expenditures have been.
    - **Sustainability Reporting:** Many Canadian businesses publish reports on their performance on environmental, social, and ethical issues. This is a key part of **triple-bottom-line reporting** (measuring social, environmental, and economic performance).
- **Informal Activities:**
    - **Organizational Culture:** The shared values and beliefs within an organization can strongly facilitate or inhibit social responsibility activities.
    - **Whistle-Blowing:** How an organization responds to employees who report wrongdoing provides insight into its true stance on social responsibility.

## 6. Ethics and CSR in Small Business

Small businesses face many of the same ethical and social responsibility issues as large corporations, but often on a more immediate and personal scale.

- **Ethical Dilemmas:** Owners may face direct requests for cash payments to "expedite" permits, deal with customers with forged IDs, or decide whether to overcharge a lax client.
- **Social Responsibility Constraints:** The decision to pursue CSR objectives, such as sponsoring local teams or making charitable donations, is often constrained by the financial flexibility of the firm. Many small business owners feel they cannot afford such initiatives.
